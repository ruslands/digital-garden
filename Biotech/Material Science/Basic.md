An¬†atom¬†is the smallest unit of matter that retains the properties of an element. It consists of three main subatomic particles:

1. Protons¬†(positively charged, found in the nucleus)
2. Neutrons¬†(neutral charge, found in the nucleus)
3. Electrons¬†(negatively charged, orbiting around the nucleus)

Structure of an Atom:

- Nucleus: The dense center of the atom containing protons and neutrons.
- Electron Cloud: A region around the nucleus where electrons move in energy levels or orbitals.

Key Properties:

- Atomic Number (Z): The number of protons in the nucleus, which defines the element (e.g., hydrogen has 1 proton, oxygen has 8).
- Mass Number (A): The total number of protons and neutrons in the nucleus.
- Electrons and Chemical Behavior: Electrons determine how atoms interact with each other to form molecules and compounds.

Atoms are the basic building blocks of all matter, making up everything from air and water to living organisms and stars. üöÄ

Ion:¬†An atom or group of atoms where the number of electrons does not equal the number of protons, resulting in a net positive or negative charge. When a stable atom gains or loses electrons, it becomes an ion.

Permselectivity: is¬†a measure of the ability of a membrane to discriminate between anions and cations.¬†

Cations are positively-charged ions (atoms or groups of atoms that have more protons than electrons due to having lost one or more electrons).¬†

Anions are negatively-charged ions¬†(meaning they have more electrons than protons due to having gained one or more electrons)

–ù—É–∫–ª–µ–æ—Ñ–∏–ª¬†(Nucleophile): A chemical species that donates an electron pair to form a chemical bond in reactions. Nucleophiles are typically rich in electrons and seek positively charged or electron-deficient sites (electrophiles) to react with.

Stern Layer (stationary layer):¬†The layer of counter-ions that adhere closely to a charged surface in an electrochemical system. This stationary layer is directly adjacent to the charged surface, where ions are strongly bound and do not move freely.

Double Layer (diffuse layer):¬†In electrochemistry, a double layer forms at the interface between a charged surface and a liquid electrolyte. The double layer consists of the Stern layer, where ions are tightly bound to the surface, and the diffuse layer, where ions are more loosely associated and diffuse further away from the surface.

Zeta Potential:¬†The electric potential at the boundary of the diffuse layer in a colloidal system. It represents the potential difference between the dispersion medium and the stationary layer of fluid attached to the particle surface, often used to measure the stability of colloidal dispersions.

Isoelectric Point (IEP):¬†The pH at which a particular molecule or surface carries no net electrical charge. At this point, the zeta potential is zero, and particles or molecules typically have minimal electrostatic repulsion, leading to aggregation or precipitation in colloidal systems.

Electrostatic Interactions:¬†Forces between charged particles or molecules. These interactions can be attractive (between opposite charges) or repulsive (between like charges) and play a key role in determining molecular structures, reactions, and the stability of colloids and solutions.

Metal-Ligand Interactions:¬†The bonding between a metal ion and a ligand, where the ligand donates one or more pairs of electrons to the metal ion, forming a coordination complex. These interactions are essential in coordination chemistry and influence the properties and stability of metal-based compounds.

Citrate-GNPs Stabilization:¬†Stabilization of gold nanoparticles (GNPs) using citrate ions, which adsorb onto the surface of the nanoparticles, providing both electrostatic and steric repulsion that prevents aggregation. This citrate layer enhances nanoparticle stability in solution.

Colloidal systems: are¬†mixtures of two or more¬†components; ¬†is a mixture in which one substance consisting of microscopically dispersed insoluble particles is suspended throughout another substance.
